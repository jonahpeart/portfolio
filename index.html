Analysing Transit and Amenity Accessibility

Winnipeg, Manitoba (March 2025)

Tech Stack: Python (geopandas, pandas, shapely, matplotlib, seaborn, H3). QGIS. ArcGISpro.

Accessibility is a crucial component when understanding the complexity of a city’s urban fabric. Many geographic and social factors lend themselves to the way one moves through a neighbourhood or around a city. Accessibility should not be viewed solely through the lens of transportation or amenities, instead, it must be understood as a combination of both to fully capture the interconnectedness of a city. In this study, Winnipeg was chosen as the case study, as they are overhauling their transportation network. This kind of acts as ‘before’ study, assuming data will be available soon after the new system is implemented.

To start mapping and analyzing, I had to collect data. This project I collected data primarily from Statistics Canada, Winnipeg's Open Data Portal, and OpenStreetMap. I used python (geopandas and pandas) to parse and prepare the data for analysis. Most of the analysis was done in QGIS, safe for the network analysis, as it was done in ArcGIS pro.

A note on spatial indexing

Rather than using census tracts or dissemination areas I opted for H3 indexing. This helped me out in both the network analysis and amenity hotspots as dissemination areas or census tracts can have irregular geography. This means that each hexagon is the same distance away from each of its respective neighbours. Spatial consistency! It does mean, however, more spatial slicing and attribute calculations in order to standardize the data.

Assessing Disturbance Patterns within the Inland Temperate Rainforest’s Core and Matrix Caribou Habitat

November 2024

Tech Stack: TerrSet (liberaGIS), QGIS Semi-Automatic Classification Plugin, USGS EarthExplorer, Copernicus Browser.

In this study I analyzed landsat imagery from 1995, 2005, 2015, and 2024. I used many different types of both unsupervised and supervised classification methods in both TerrSet liberaGIS and QGIS. I examined the different spectral signatures of the different training sites to ensure spectral consistency across the years. Though this project was not exhaustive it did provide insightful results.

The Urban Heat island effect

March 2025

Tech Stack: QGIS, ArcGISpro, USGS EarthExplorer.

This was a part of a larger study with other collaborators for a Climate Policy course. My focus was on analysing the effect of a small informal green space on the urban heat island effect in Montreal. In addition, the location of the green space is situated in a unique spot as it acts as a funnel from the metro to the Mile End area. Incidentally, this area is where the large employer, Ubisoft Montreal, is located. This urban 'funnel' helped increase people's contact with nature, and ideally, their connectedness with it. QGIS and ArcGIS pro were used for this study.

Taking Back My Data: Rethinking The Basemap

December 2024

Tech Stack: Python (pandas, GDAL, folium, matplotlib), QGIS, HTML, CSS, JS (leaflet), GIMP.

Since 2017 I have been using google maps to track my location. 135,000 lines of data, stories, patterns littered throughout. Facially, it's all very structured, rigid, and impersonal. Data, data, data, what a dilemma. How can I show my stories? How do I show it without any labels or unrelated data? Well to start, rethink the basemap. I’ll make my own.

Above is a gif showing two maps. The two spaces (cities) I spent my time in between 2017 and 2021. There are small small notes and drawings associated with places of meaning. The question marks reveal photos taken in that spot. They might seem irrelevant, but they have stories... liminal spaces.
